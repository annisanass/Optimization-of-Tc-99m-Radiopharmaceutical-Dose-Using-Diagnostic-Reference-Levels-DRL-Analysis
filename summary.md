# Project Summary

This project focuses on evaluating radiopharmaceutical dose administration in nuclear medicine procedures through a comparison with national Diagnostic Reference Levels (DRL). The study aims to assess whether the administered activity levels follow recommended radiation safety standards and to identify opportunities for dose optimization in clinical practice.

The analysis is based on collected data from multiple nuclear medicine procedures conducted between 2022 and 2024. The procedures include Myocardial Perfusion Scan (SPM) and SPM Viability using Tc-99m MIBI, Bone Scan using Tc-99m MDP, GFR and Renogram using Tc-99m DTPA, Whole Body Scan (WBS), and Thyroid Scan using Tc-99m Pertechnetate. For each procedure, the typical administered dose (in MBq) was calculated and compared with the Indonesian Diagnostic Reference Levels (I-DRL).

The results demonstrate variability in dose administration practices across different procedures. In myocardial perfusion scans (SPM), the administered doses in both 2023 and 2024 exceeded the recommended DRL values, indicating a potential risk of unnecessary radiation exposure and highlighting the need for optimization. In contrast, SPM viability studies were found to be within acceptable DRL limits, suggesting appropriate dose management.

For bone scan procedures, the administered doses consistently remained below DRL values from 2022 to 2024, reflecting effective optimization and adherence to radiation safety principles. Similarly, Whole Body Scan (WBS) procedures also showed doses below DRL limits, indicating conservative and controlled radiopharmaceutical usage. The GFR and renogram procedures were observed to be within the recommended range, demonstrating compliance with established standards.

Thyroid scan procedures showed a slight deviation in 2023, where the administered dose exceeded the DRL value; however, improvements were observed in 2024, with doses reduced to within acceptable limits. This indicates a positive adjustment toward better dose optimization practices.

Overall, this study highlights the importance of continuous monitoring and evaluation of radiopharmaceutical doses in nuclear medicine. By benchmarking against DRL standards, healthcare providers can identify inconsistencies, reduce unnecessary radiation exposure, and improve patient safety. The findings emphasize the role of DRL as an essential tool for quality assurance and optimization in medical imaging, supporting the implementation of radiation protection principles in clinical environments.
